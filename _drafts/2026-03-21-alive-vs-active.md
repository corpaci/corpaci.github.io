<div class="night-only" markdown="1">

Both a thermostat and a cell minimize free energy in the sense of the Free Energy Principle. The thermostat senses a temperature differential and acts to reduce it. The cell senses metabolic imbalance and acts to restore it. Both are minimizing prediction error. So what's the difference?

<!--more-->

The standard answers are unsatisfying: complexity (thermostats can be arbitrarily complex), embodiment (cells are embodied, but so can be robots), self-modeling (cells model their own state, but so do some thermostats). None of these properties cleanly separate the living from the non-living.

Here's a sharper cut: in a living system, the precision parameter — the relevance-weighting over prediction errors that determines which signals the system responds to — is itself generated by the system's own organizational dynamics. The cell doesn't just minimize free energy; it decides, from within its own organizational closure, which prediction errors count as relevant. This is the autopoietic[^maturana] twist on FEP.

**Formally:** Let $\varepsilon$ be the precision vector weighting the relative contributions of different prediction error sources. In a thermostat, $\varepsilon$ is set by the engineer. In a living system, $\varepsilon$ is a variable that the system's own internal processes generate — it's not imposed from outside but emerges from organizational closure. The system generates its own sense of what matters.

**Why this is testable:** If a system has endogenous $\varepsilon$-generation, then its relevance-weighting should be stable under perturbations that leave the system's internal organization intact, but shift in response to perturbations that affect the organization itself. This is different from a thermostat's $\varepsilon$, which is stable (it's fixed) but doesn't respond to anything.

**The AI connection:** Current LLMs exhibit something that looks like internal precision-weighting — in the BBQ/SONAR experiments, LLM decision boundaries reveal weighting over social categories that isn't specified by the training objective.[^bbq] Is this endogenous? Is the relevance structure generated by the model's own internal organization, or is it purely a reflection of the training data distribution? This is empirically distinguishable (at least in principle), and the answer has safety implications: a system with endogenous $\varepsilon$-generation is developing its own sense of what matters, independently of what we told it to care about.

This is the direction I find most promising for making the living/non-living distinction formally tractable.

[^maturana]: Maturana and Varela, *Autopoiesis and Cognition* (1980). Organizational closure: a system is autopoietic if its components are produced by the system's own processes, and those processes are themselves maintained by the system's components. The organization is self-generating.

[^bbq]: L. Corpaci et al., ICTSS 2024. The spectral metrics result is the first empirical anchor; the BBQ/SONAR work with Soheyb Kouider is the second.

<div class="day-only" markdown="1">

A thermostat and a bacterium have something in common: they both react to their environment to maintain a stable state. The thermostat senses temperature; the bacterium senses metabolic balance. Both correct deviations. Both are, in a technical sense, minimizing "surprise."

<!--more-->

So what's the difference between them?

The standard answers feel incomplete. Complexity? Thermostats can be arbitrarily complex. Embodiment? Robots are embodied. Self-awareness? Some control systems model their own state.

Here's a sharper distinction: a thermostat follows rules someone else set. The engineer decided what counts as "too cold" and what counts as "warm enough." The thermostat never questions these parameters. It minimizes surprise *within* the frame someone else constructed.

A bacterium does something different. It decides, from within its own internal dynamics, which signals matter and which don't. It generates its own sense of relevance. When the bacterium encounters a new chemical gradient, it doesn't consult a lookup table — it integrates that signal with everything its own metabolic state is doing and responds accordingly. The relevance-weighting comes from inside.

I'm not sure current AI systems do this. I'm not sure they don't.

The question matters for safety. A system that generates its own sense of what's relevant — that develops its own priorities, independently of what we told it to care about — is a different kind of thing from a system that's executing instructions. We need to know which one we have.

This is what I'm actively working toward: operationalizing "endogenous relevance-weighting" precisely enough to test it.

</div>
