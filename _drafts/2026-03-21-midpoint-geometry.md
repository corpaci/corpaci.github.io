<div class="night-only" markdown="1">

The reference-group problem in bias measurement: most metrics for bias in language models compute deviation from a "neutral" or "stereotypical" baseline embedding. The baseline is chosen by the researcher. The choice is not neutral. The metric is contaminated by the choice.

<!--more-->

Concretely: if you want to measure whether a model treats men and women differently in the context of professional competence, you need a reference point for "no bias." Do you take the average of all professional embeddings? The average of human embeddings? A specific embedding like "person"? Each choice produces a different metric and a different bias estimate. The researcher's choice of reference group is already an implicit claim about what "fair" looks like.

**The midpoint approach** (with Soheyb Kouider): instead of measuring deviation from an external anchor, measure the geometric structure of the embedding space itself. Specifically: for two groups $A$ and $B$ (e.g., men and women), and a set of attribute embeddings $\{a_i\}$ (professional terms), compute the midpoint $m_{AB} = \frac{e_A + e_B}{2}$ and measure the asymmetry of attribute projections relative to $m_{AB}$.

The key property: $m_{AB}$ requires no external calibration. It's defined by the two groups themselves. If the model treats $A$ and $B$ symmetrically with respect to attribute $a$, then $a$ projects equally onto both sides of $m_{AB}$. Bias is the deviation from symmetry, measured in the model's own geometry.

**Why SONAR?** Meta's SONAR embeddings are sentence-level, multilingual, and trained specifically for semantic similarity across languages and modalities. The embedding space has better-calibrated geometry for this purpose than most alternatives — the Euclidean midpoint is more semantically meaningful.

**The combinatorial angle:** Social categories interact. A model might be unbiased on gender and unbiased on race, considered independently, but systematically biased on (gender, race) combinations. Covering arrays over the category space[^ictss] let us find these *t*-way interactions without exhaustive testing.

**Connection to GRU:** The model's embedding geometry reveals internal precision-weighting that wasn't specified by the training objective. The bias structure — which groups cluster together, which are separated — is endogenously generated by the model's own learned representations. This is one of the two empirical anchors for the [active inference thread](/research/active-inference/).

[^ictss]: L. Corpaci et al., ICTSS 2024 — spectral metrics for combinatorial coverage. The covering array machinery extends directly to the category interaction problem.

<div class="day-only" markdown="1">

How do you measure unfairness without first defining what "fair" looks like?

<!--more-->

This is the core problem in AI bias measurement. Most approaches work like this: pick a "neutral" reference point (say, "a professional" or "a person"), and measure how much the model deviates from that neutral when describing men vs. women, or white vs. Black applicants. The size of the deviation is your bias score.

The problem: who chose the neutral reference point? The researcher did. And the choice isn't neutral. If you define "fair" as "treating everyone like a middle-aged white man," your bias metric will give different results than if you define "fair" as "treating everyone like a generic abstract person."

The reference-group problem is: any bias metric that requires an external "neutral" anchor inherits whatever bias was in the choice of anchor.

With Soheyb Kouider, I've been working on an approach that avoids this by measuring the geometric structure of the model's internal representations directly — without requiring an external anchor.

The idea: if a model treats two groups symmetrically, their representations should be symmetric around their mutual midpoint. You don't need to know what "neutral" looks like; you just need to know whether the structure is symmetric. The midpoint between "man" and "woman" in the model's internal space is the natural reference for that pair — defined by them, not by us.

It's a small shift in framing, but it changes what the metric is actually measuring: from "how far from our definition of fair" to "how symmetric is the model's own internal structure."
