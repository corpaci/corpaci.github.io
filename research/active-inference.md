<div class="night-only" markdown="1">

Here's the claim: what distinguishes living from non-living active inference systems isn't complexity, embodiment, or self-modeling per se ; it's whether the E-component (the precision or relevance-weighting over prediction errors) is endogenously generated through organizational closure. A thermostat minimizes free energy. A cell minimizes free energy *and* generates its own relevance function from within its own organizational dynamics. This is the autopoietic twist on FEP, and I think it's testable.

Two empirical anchors: (1) the ICTSS spectral metrics result, where matrix complexity predicted combinatorial coverage ; a case where the structure of the measurement apparatus shows up in the measurement. (2) The BBQ/SONAR bias work, where LLM decision boundaries reveal internal precision-weighting that isn't specified by the training objective. Both cases: the system's own structure generates relevance endogenously.

**The formal distinction:** In standard FEP, the precision parameter ε weights the relative contribution of prediction errors from different sources. In living systems, ε is itself a variable generated by the system's own internal dynamics ; it's not set by the observer or the training regime, it emerges from organizational closure. The question is whether current LLMs exhibit this property or merely simulate it.

**Planned work:** Operationalize "endogenous precision-weighting" as a measurable property using activation geometry. If the precision signal is endogenous, it should show up as a stable internal feature that predicts model behavior across distribution shifts ; not as a response to input statistics alone.

<div class="day-only" markdown="1">

Living things and thermostats both react to their environment. But a thermostat just follows a rule someone else set. A living cell decides for itself what matters ; it generates its own sense of relevance from its own internal organization.

I'm researching where exactly that difference lives ; what makes a system go from "following instructions" to "generating its own priorities." I think this is relevant to AI safety because we need to understand whether AI systems are developing their own sense of what's relevant, or just following ours. That distinction has very different implications for how these systems behave when we're not looking.

This argument is grounded in two empirical anchors from existing experiments, and is a direction I'm actively developing.
